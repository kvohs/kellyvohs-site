Sunday Letter — No. 176 — September 21, 2025

Who's Governing You?

Every September 17th I think about the day we lost our house.

It was 1989 and a hurricane ended up being more than predicted. We were in St. Croix. We started the night sitting in the living room. We thought we’d sit tight. Morning would come. A little clean up and things would get back to normal. That’s the way it usually went with these storms.

Then we heard a crack, looked up, and the roof was gone. We made our way to the kitchen and huddled behind the fridge. The cabinets flew open. Everything started swirling. It was a “kitchen tornado.” We made it to the bedroom, crawled under the bed, and waited it out.

Walking out the next day was surreal. Like a nuclear weapon went off. The apartments on the hill were gone, trees were down everywhere, and it seemed like the grass blew out the ground in places. But the wind was calm. Coming from the right. Warm, as usual. It smelled green in that post-storm way. I looked to my left and my step-father was looking at pieces of a house, maybe ours, that were embedded in the engine of our car. We walked down the dirt drive. I’d never go back.

My mom and I always talk on September 17th. Not this year. The first time we haven’t.

After we’d hang up, I always drift back to those 13 years. It’s strange how memory works. I remember mostly the good things. Salt. A wet dog. Playing under the porch. Forgetting easter eggs in my lunch box for all of spring break when I was 9. She never let me forget that but always with a smile.

Eventually my memories end up on a little green sailboat. I grew up under that white sail. On most trips it was just me and that wet dog. Magical. Or maybe that’s just memory.

This year I kept thinking about the times I’d lose control on that boat. The boom would whip around. Sometimes I’d capsize. Or other times I’d not turn hard enough and get stuck facing into the wind, sails useless.

Usually, I wasn’t paying attention, not intentional, or not decisive enough. Life’s the same. Stop paying attention, something else steps in. The tide. The wind. Emotions. Another perspective. You aren’t steering, someone—or something— else is.

I used to think it was discipline that mattered most. But discipline is just self-control. It’s tactical. In the moment decisions. Hit snooze or go for a run. Lash out at your partner or pause and respond.

Self-governance is deeper. It’s strategic. It’s the beliefs that drive your everyday decisions. Go for that run because you want to be around, and healthy, with your partner to play with the grand kids one day.

Many of our beliefs are inherited. Passed down or acquired in some way. There is wisdom in what we inherit. Not every lesson has to be learned with our own pain. Accept them intentionally. Consciously.

Take “work life balance.” It sounds reasonable. Recharge. Set boundaries. Be “responsible.” You might produce good work following the balance framework. But then someone else figures out how to produce exceptional work by making their own script with work-life integration.

And to be clear, I find time to recharge, love solitude, be with my people, walk around with my camera, and use my OOO. But it’s my version, and probably not for everyone.

It’s easy to pick up the default. The narratives from media, friends, and parents. The “common sense” assumptions about what’s appropriate, realistic, or true. That’s all inherited governance. Someone else’s operating system running in your head. Someone else telling you what to do instead of you learning to read the wind.

The moment you stop examining your beliefs, external forces govern. You aren’t holding the tiller anymore. Someone else is. Often they are not malicious. It’s just inevitable. And now you’re in a different kind of storm.

Discipline doesn’t matter if you are not clear about your why, success, truth, and what is really important. We need discipline and self-governance but they’re just steps on the way to something more: sovereignty. And I don’t think you find satisfaction without all three.

Take care. Be good.

Kelly
