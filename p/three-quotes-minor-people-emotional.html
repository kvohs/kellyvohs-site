Sunday Letter — No. 181 — October 26, 2025

Three Quotes: Minor People, Emotional Decisions, and Smooth Answers

I came across these three quotes this week. They’ve led to some uncomfortable but important questions. Maybe they’ll do the same for you.

On Time, Energy and People

“It’s easy to remain mediocre. All you need to do is spend major time on minor things with minor people.” - Jim Rohn

Minor people often appear big, and big people often appear minor. Where do I miss that and spend my time with the wrong people? Who energizes me and who drains me?

Often we think time is the most important resource, I think it might be energy. People who energize us multiply us, those who drain us reduce what we can get done in the same time.

On Rationality vs. Emotional Decision-Making

“You like to imagine yourself in control of your fate, consciously planning the course of your life the best you can. But you are largely unaware how your emotions dominate you. They make you veer towards ideas that soothe your ego, They make you see what you want to see, depending on your mood, and this disconnect from reality is the source of the bad decisions and negative patterns that haunt your life. Rationality is the ability to counteract these emotional efforts, to think instead of react. To open your mind to what’s really happening, as opposed to what you are feeling. It does not come naturally. It is a power we must cultivate, but in doing so we realize our greatest potential.” - Robert Greene

I had to read that a couple of times. How many decisions are based on defending myself or idea of myself and I don’t even realize it? Where would my life be simpler, more honest, and maybe peaceful if I could move from emotional to rational.

Maybe those areas where I find myself justifying something disproportionately are flags to dig deeper. Places where I build the most elaborate explanations might be precisely where I’m avoiding a simpler truth.

On Deep Understanding

“To read carefully and not be satisfied with a rough understanding of the whole, and not to agree too quickly with those who have a lot to say about something.” - Marcus Aurelius

Where do I need to dig deeper to understand? A problem, a perspective, my misconception. When do I accept a smooth, or rehearsed, answer too quickly? Then, when do I have enough information to make a decision and go?

Sometimes the most persuasive voices are the ones we should question more. And then at some point, have the courage to decide, take responsibility, and go. But always hold decisions lightly. “It’s the truth I’m after. When the facts change, I’ll change.”

Take care. Be good.
-Kelly
