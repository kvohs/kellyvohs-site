Sunday Letter — No. 195 — April 26, 2026

Steps

A friend told me about his running goal this year. He’s decided to run a certain number of miles over the year. I liked it. So I’m doing it too. It’s long but forces consistency. Long consistency. He’s a clear thinker. Glad he told me.

I was adding a few more miles earlier this week, running through a town I spent a couple years in as a teenager. The crush who lived over there. The roman candles down that street. That time my buddies and I did that thing we all promised never to tell anyone.

Took a left down a long sandy road that leads to a little dock. Cool morning. Red wing blackbirds are back. A new lightweight running jacket I love. A random podcast just to switch it up.

They talked about a novel way of getting things done. Backwards from how most of the world thinks.

Here it is.

Step 1: Make the requirements less dumb.

Question every requirement. It’s easy to add things and rationalize why they’re required. Especially when “smart” people add them. Those are harder to question. But we have to.

It gets worse in big organizations. Requirements start coming from a department or a team. Not a human. No one person is responsible. Sometimes they even become political.

Every requirement gets a name attached. An actual human who is asking for it.

Step 2: Delete. Delete. Delete.

Smart people add things. Then they add more. Features, parts, steps in the process. The most important key on the keyboard is delete.

Strip it all away. What is the minimum we need for this to work?

If you aren’t adding back 10% because you deleted too much, you didn’t actually do this step.

Step 3: Simplify and optimize.

The order of this one is what matters most. Often we simplify or optimize something that shouldn’t exist in the first place. Now you’ve made the wrong thing efficient. That’s worse than slow.

Step 4: Accelerate.

In the special operations community there’s an underlying belief: “You can do 40% more than you think.” I’ve heard it attributed to Navy SEALs.

You think you can only carry so much or run so far, but when it really matters, you figure it out. When people are counting on you, the rational you goes in a little box. The real you comes to the fight.

Usually, we set timelines that everyone agrees are “realistic” and “reasonable.” Human nature is to use all of the time allotted. Like the big project at school. The work expands to fit the time.

So compress the time. If the mission matters, and the people care, they’ll figure it out. Maybe you miss. That should be okay when you’ve gone after it with everything you have.

Step 5: Automate.

This is often where people start. I’ve made that mistake. We automate things that shouldn’t exist. Now you’re scaling waste. Or worse, dysfunction.

The creator of these steps made this mistake in a big way. He automated the entire factory. Wrong things. Things that weren’t even required. When he figured it out he ripped the robots out and started over. An expensive mistake that almost cost him the company.

It was Elon Musk. You don’t have to want to be him. Most don’t. Or even like him or what he’s doing. But the process stands on its own.

The dock is better than it was when I was a kid. The pilings are new. Some parts now rise and fall with the tide. I forgot about the tide. Low tide is so different. And interesting. I love how some birds can just walk over the mud and make their way.

Take care. Be good. -Kelly
