How I AI

Hi friend,

I've been asked by a few friends "How I AI?" I thought I'd make this for anyone interested. It's not everything that is possible or even everything I do. It's just my take and meant to be helpful for anyone that might need help getting started. I'm always happy to help more, just email or text me. Let's get into it.

What do you use?

I've used most of the models. Well, most. GPT, Claude, Gemini, Mistral, Gemma, Llama, and the list goes on. Today, I almost exclusively use Claude / Anthropic. But before we go further, I feel obligated to say that I think AI may be a threat to our species. A real threat. Ironically, it also may be the reason we survive. I don't know how all of this plays out.

What do you do?

Well, lots. AI serves as a chief of staff, an engineer (the software kind), an admin, a shopper, and greco-roman long dead dude translator.

Today I can make software that I've always wanted to build. Not perfectly, to be sure, but it works. It gets things wrong. Builds things that a better engineer would have borrowed (open source) or bought. But I work through that and have three pieces of software, including what I'm writing this on, that I'm proud of.

Then there are all the questions and problems in my life I'm trying to solve. From how do I replace the hose bib to being the executor of an estate to getting to the things in my inbox that matter while avoiding the noise.

Start by thinking of it as a member of your team

If I had to point to the one place people get wrong, it's this. Often friends treat AI like a search box: put in a request, take answer out. Don't. You can, but you'd be missing it.

Start with, "I've got a new person on my team. They know marketing, finance, and residential spigot repair." It was trained on human data so it's going to have biases, tendencies, strengths, and make mistakes.

So, brief it the way you'd brief a sharp new teammate. Tell it what you're trying to get done and what the end state looks like, then let it work out the how. The way I actually talk to it goes something like this: "Hey, I'm trying to do X. Here's how I'm thinking about it. Here's what I'd like you to do. If you have a better way, or ideas, I'm all ears. There are a few things that really matter to me like X, Y, and Z. Otherwise, I'm flexible. Best idea wins." Sound familiar? Some days you hold their hand, some days they surprise you, and other days you want to scream. But like any good teammate, they get better with you. And like a teammate, the more clear you are with them on what you want and need the better they will perform.

Two things that matter more than you'd think

Talk to it. You don't have to type. I'm a pretty fast typer, but not faster than I talk. I dictate almost everything. I use an app (monologue) or you can use what's built into Claude. Sometimes I turn on voice mode and we banter on a subject. It feels strange for about a day. Or two. And I don't like talking to it before my second cup of coffee, but I don't like humans before then either. Eventually, it will be normal.

The real point is you can talk the way you'd talk to a person. Ramble, double back, make mistakes, and don't bother cleaning any of it up. It's fast, you don't have to be tidy. And if you do type, don't fix your typos.

If you're trying to learn something or research something just talk it out on your drive. And before you go there, I'm not suggesting it replace humans or my therapist. It's just available to talk about my obscure need to understand why basil helps keep the bugs off my tomato plants. I'll take it.

The other night I was reading a very dense book full of quotes by long dead guys. It was english, but so old that my feeble brain couldn't understand it. So I turned on voice mode, read the quote to it, and asked, "what are they actually trying to say here?"

And don't worry. The more you tell it, the more context it has. Usually, better results. Don't trim it down. Dump the whole mess that is your problem, who you are, what you are trying to do, what you've already tried, and all the detail you would leave out because you get tired of typing. It does more, with more. And you can ask it to "brief back" so you make sure it understands.

Things you could try today

Dinner. Take a photo of a bunch of ingredients and ask, "What would Nigel Slater do?" The first time I did this it responded with "First, Nigel would open a bottle of wine..." Ok, you have me.

Making a decision with too many options. I wanted a new e-ink reading tablet, and there are a dizzying number of them. I had it build comparison tables across the things I actually cared about (screen size, weight, width, price, all of it) until the right choice was obvious. Then get me the links to each with best price.

Solving real-life problems. A spigot at the house broke and I had no idea how to fix it. I took a picture, talked it through, and AI helped me map out the solution, price the parts, and drop them into a Home Depot basket. Problem to plan to cart.

Quick visualizations. When I need to see an idea, I'll have it build a little web page or chart in a few minutes instead of laboring over a whole PowerPoint.

A reading companion. It uses my book highlights to point me toward what to read next, sometimes right in my lane, sometimes the opposite on purpose. And when I'm reading something dense, I can stop and ask for a quick plain-English perspective. This habit is what led me to build Dispatch, below.

A morning briefing. Every morning I get an AI-written rundown: my calendar, what matters most that day, how the business is doing. And it doesn't just report. It tees up the next step, like drafting a reply, writing a quick prep note, or blocking focus time.

Inbox triage. Instead of drowning in email, something scans it all day and only taps me when a real person is actually waiting on me. Otherwise it stays quiet.

Knowing the people I'm about to meet. Before a meeting I get a quick, researched read on who I'm talking to. Context I'd otherwise forget or never dig up.

Tracking what's owed, in both directions. Not just my to-do list, but what I owe people and what people owe me, pulled out of normal conversation and chased down so things don't slip.

Meeting transcripts. Conversations get captured and summarized (Granola, Zoom, Notion, etc.), with the real decisions and follow-ups pulled out, so I can be present in the room instead of scribbling notes.

Things I built (you don't need to)

Coop. My own chief of staff, hand-rolled from nothing, with its own little database behind it. It's the thing quietly running a lot of what I described above. To be clear, you could get most of the way there just by connecting your tools to Claude. I built mine because I wanted to understand how it all works.

Dispatch. A quiet place to study what you've read. It syncs your Readwise highlights, then helps you note them, reshape them, and hear them read back. The opposite of an endless feed: just the books you chose, the lines you kept, and the time to actually think them through.

Type. A tiny writing app, on Mac and now iPhone, that's one sheet of paper. You type, and what you typed is locked. No backspace into committed lines. When you're done, you keep it (saved as plain markdown) or you burn it, and it's gone. It's for morning pages, throwaway drafts, and getting a thought out of your head. Private by design: nothing leaves your computer.

What I don't use AI for

Some of what I don't use it for matters just as much.

I don't use AI to write. I've tried. 1) I don't think we should outsource art 2) Writing brings clarity. I don't want to outsource that either.

I will use it as an Editor: Please review this as an editor. Do not make edits. You are an editor for a major publishing house. I want clarity, where I drift, where I have darlings that need to be killed, grammar, and ideas that I might have missed. There's nobody around at 6 a.m. to read my stuff before it goes out, and it fills that seat.

If you are still reading, start

Get Claude. Pay for a plan. Don't be cheap. It's a couple of the unnaturally colored refreshers at Starbucks a month. Any paid plan gets you Cowork in the desktop app (Pro, at $20 a month, is plenty to begin), and that's where most of this lives for most people.

Make it your first stop

Ask one question first: is there a way AI can help me do this faster, or better, or just do it for me?

Do that consistently and it adds up. It frees up the time and energy for the work only you can do. The work that needs your judgment and craft.

And if you don't know how to do something, just ask it. Not "where can I find a tutorial," but "I'm trying to do this thing, walk me through it." You can tell it to slow down. Say "I'm not technical, explain it simply, step by step, and don't assume I know anything." It won't judge you, and it will never send you off to go watch someone else's video. That alone changes everything.

Tell it how you want it to talk to you

This was a small change that made a big difference. You can tell Claude how you want it to behave, and it remembers. I've told mine: don't give me prose, give me simple, direct answers. Don't be a sycophant. Be truthful and honest, even when it isn't what I want to hear.

It won't be perfect. Sometimes it drifts back into flattery or fluff and I have to point it back. That's a little annoying, but it's also kind of human, and a quick "hey, you're doing it again" usually sets it straight.

One thing that makes me a little sad

The hardest part is watching big organizations. So many of them can't figure out how to put these tools in their teams' hands, citing security, citing cost, citing a dozen understandable reasons. But here's the thing that actually frustrates me: with a business or enterprise account, the major AI companies generally don't train on your data. The specifics vary from one to the next, so it's worth checking, but the biggest objection mostly isn't what people fear it is.

So if you run a big company, my honest plea is to get out of your own way and give your people the tools. I'd bet a lot of them are already buying their own laptops and figuring it out on the side. Help them. Because used well, downsides aside, this stuff frees people up to do the things only people can do. Ironically, it can help us be more human, not less.

That's most of it. If even one of these makes a week of yours a little lighter, it was worth writing down.

Onward,
Kelly

A few things you can go try now

Here are some prompts you can copy and paste straight into Claude. Edit the bits in brackets. And remember, you don't have to type any of these. You can just say them out loud.

One honest heads-up first: most of these you can do in a normal chat, even by voice on your phone. A couple (the morning briefing, the inbox triage) need the desktop app with your email and calendar connected, and work best set up as a scheduled task so they run on their own. None of that is hard. If something doesn't work, just tell Claude what you were trying to do and ask it to fix it or set it up properly. It will.

Brand new and not sure where to begin:

I'm brand new to Cowork and not sure what it can do for me. Ask me a few questions about my work and my week, then suggest a handful of things you could take off my plate. Pick the easiest, most useful one, and let's actually do it together right now, step by step.

Set up a morning briefing:

Act as my chief of staff. Set this up as a scheduled task that runs every weekday at 6 a.m. Each morning, give me a short rundown of my day: what's on my calendar, what matters most, and anyone I owe a reply to. You'll need my email and calendar connected, so walk me through that first if they aren't.

Triage your inbox:

Go through my inbox and show me only the emails where a real person is actually waiting on me to reply. Skip newsletters, receipts, and anything automated. For each one, tell me who it's from, what they need, and how long it's been waiting. Once it's working, set it up to run a few times a day on its own.

You'll have to hook up your inbox first. Again, just tell Claude that's what you want to do and it'll walk you through it. You might only be able to connect a personal account for now, if your work email is still in the way.

Prep for a meeting:

I have a meeting with [name] at [company] soon. Find what you can about them and the company, and give me a short, plain-English brief so I walk in prepared. Share a link to anything they've published recently.

Understand something you're reading:

I'm reading [book or article] and this part isn't clicking: [paste it, or just read it aloud, or take a photo]. Explain it to me simply.

Find your next book:

Here are some books I've loved: [list them]. Suggest three I'd probably like, and one that challenges how I think. Tell me why for each.

Choose between too many options:

I'm trying to pick a [product, e.g. an e-ink reading tablet] and there are way too many options. First, ask me a few questions about what I actually care about. Then build me a comparison table of the main ones across what matters, and tell me which you'd pick for me, and why.

See an idea instead of building a deck:

Here's some data: [paste it]. Make me a simple web page with a clear chart I can screenshot. I don't need a slideshow. Show me a local HTML.

Make sense of a report someone sent you:

Here's a [report or slide deck] someone sent me about [topic, e.g. my hotel's performance]: [paste the whole thing, or upload the file]. Pull out the three most important trends, and give me three sharp questions I should ask about it.

Fix something around the house:

My [thing] is broken. Here's a photo and what's happening: [describe it]. Walk me through the fix step by step, list the parts I'd need, and roughly what they cost.

And the one to keep in your back pocket whenever you feel lost:

I'm not technical. Walk me through this one step at a time, in plain language, and don't assume I know anything. If I need to click something, tell me exactly where it is. Don't send me to a video, just show me here.

Honestly, that last one will get you through almost anything. You don't need to know the magic words. You just need to be willing to say "I don't get it, try again."
