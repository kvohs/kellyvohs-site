That’s the version most people write. But the truth is people aren’t resumes. They aren’t titles. They’re stories. So, if we sat for coffee and asked to hear mine, it would go something like this. ↓

Over coffee.

We were driving through Baghdad. My buddy's right wrist was draped over the wheel. We were in the old grey SUV. The one with the gold pinstripe. Crack in the windshield. It was my favorite. Lighter. Then he looked over and said, "Dude, stop."

He was right. This was combat. Not war. My grandad went to war. People were barely making it a few hundred meters away. I was complaining about the bureaucracy, the red tape, the food. I had a chow hall, a hot shower, and coffee every morning. Looking back, I'm embarrassed.

But I'm getting ahead of myself.

I grew up in the U.S. Virgin Islands. Island life. Everything you can imagine. A kid and his dog. Rarely any shoes. And hurricanes. Hugo took our house when I was 12. I'd spend sixth grade on the next island over with my dad. My first job was as a dishwasher at his restaurant, the Delly Deck. I made $340 something. I bought a camera.

At 18 I walked into The Breakers of Palm Beach as a server. Eventually I got my first shot at leading. It wasn't perfect. I almost got fired. Twice. Learned hard lessons. Got a shot to build a restaurant called L'Escalier from the ground up and we won the AAA Five Diamond award with an incredible team.

The Breakers taught me that it's all about people. Build the team, give them the expectations, processes, voice, and care, and you will find that team has superpowers. I can still hear the click of the heat lamp as the chef turns it on. The glance from a teammate across the room. The almost indistinguishable nod to the table we needed to clear. The words that didn't need to be said. Some of my best friends to this day. Including my adventure partner, Brandi.

Two weeks after we invaded Iraq, at 26, I enlisted in the U.S. Army. 18X, the Special Forces pipeline. With a motto of De Oppresso Liber, free the oppressed, I was all in. A need to serve, a bit of an adventure deficit, and a romantic idea of war, and off I went. After two years of selection and training, I earned the Green Beret. Four deployments to Iraq and Central Asia. I was incredibly lucky to work with amazing men and women. In Baghdad, Kurdistan, Tajikistan, Afghanistan, and some other stans. With people from every agency and a dozen countries. Relationships I'll never forget.

That's where I came to truly understand this idea of we, not me. And usually there are worse things happening to better people. When we accept that we're really all just making it up as we go, you can check your ego. Humans. Strengths. Weaknesses. Hopes. Dreams. Together.

From then on it was clear to me that I must do things with people that matter to me and work on things that matter. Mission. With a team.

Brandi and I had three boys during those years. I loved the mission but I couldn't be a husband and a father and a Green Beret. Some guys can. I couldn't. I'd been mostly gone for five years.

I was in Tajikistan when an old teammate from L'Escalier called. His French accent, the paisley pattern of the sofa in the house we rented in Dushanbe. He had a job for me. What time is it in Colorado. I need to call Brandi.

VP of Operations at BR Guest Hospitality in New York. Twenty-plus restaurants, three thousand people. Lots of restaurants. I'd been out of the game for almost a decade. Now I was in arguably the most competitive restaurant market in the world. The pressure was real. The transition was so important to us as a family. Brandi reminds me of the PTSD. The kids were small. Those times were tough. Just like the army, Brandi was so good. I learned so much. And very proud of that team.

Every role I've taken has come from a relationship. Even the army. In a way. My grandfather was a Colonel. WWII, Korea, Vietnam. Retired to a small farm in the Blue Ridge Mountains. Volunteer fire department. Married 65 years. Grandkids running on green hills and eating tomatoes like apples. I think I'm just trying to be him when I grow up.

A close friend called me to leave BR Guest and come run the Boca Raton Resort & Club. Our families had always been close. He'd known me since the Breakers days. Since I was 18. A chance to do something together wasn't something Brandi and I could turn down. Hilton property, Blackstone portfolio company. Two hotels, 1,047 rooms. 14 restaurants.

39 days in. A Sunday. I was sitting on my office floor looking up at my blue handwriting. 67 projects on a whiteboard. We need to do these if we want to win. My friend told me to do nothing for 90 days. But there I was. Palm trees outside. The valets opening and closing doors for guests. The pressure hit me. I could feel my heart beating. I didn't want to let my friend down. I didn't want to let the team down. There was no way we could do all of this.

Then I said out loud: "do what you can every day." If we get 1% better each day, it compounds. That's more than 3,600% better in a year. Calm, steady progress. That became Better Today Than Yesterday (we pronounce it "Betty"). That's how I try to run my life now. I write about that each week here. Just get a little better. And help others do the same if you can.

Chicago was next. Another adventure. Hilton asked me to transfer. To build and open the Conrad. I ran the Waldorf Astoria Chicago while we built the Conrad. Getting to learn and understand construction like that up close and personal is not something I'd trade. The blue painters tape around our wrists. Walking rooms, tagging punch items, installing systems, hiring humans. Growing from 1 person to a team of 250. Then, opening day. The team was so proud.

Blackstone asked us to move back to New York. StuyTown. The largest rental community in the country. 11,241 apartments. A city in a city. We turned it into Beam Living and grew it to 15,000 apartments across New York.

In August 2020, middle of COVID, they asked me to also run LivCor, their national multifamily platform. Over the next four years we grew LivCor from 40,000 to 175,000 apartments. Evictions were happening everywhere but we didn't do that. Not one eviction during COVID. For two years. These are people's homes. Where babies come home, birthdays are celebrated, and hard days are felt. Around that dinner table. We built a team of Good Neighbors. Kind. Helpful. Thoughtful. Respectful. I think we showed that you can do well and do good at the same time. An incredible team and time.

My time with Blackstone, over 12 years, in the room with some of the smartest people I've ever worked with. And people that really cared about doing the right thing. We always tried to do the right thing. For our guests, residents, and team.

An opportunity with an old friend came up. SafetyCulture. I'd been a customer since 2012. A global platform that helps frontline teams get better every day. These are the people that make the world work that most people don't remember. They make things, move things, and serve things. Building software for the workforce, not the deskforce. Blackstone didn't have something like this they could offer but said "if it's a dumpster fire, come back." Brandi and I decided to give it a shot.

The role was supposed to be President, based in the U.S. Two months before my start date the founder called and said he couldn't be CEO anymore. "It's yours."

25,000 customers in 180+ countries and teams across APAC, EMEA, and North America. After a year on the road, 250,000 miles, and a platform that needed a full rebuild, it was obvious they needed a CEO in Sydney full time. That's where 500 of the 800 people are. We did that in the army and it wasn't something I would do again. My relationships at home will always be my priority. The team needed someone there every day. Hard decision, but easy decision. I'll miss the team and the mission.

Like most people I'm proud of my family. But that's a throwaway. You're supposed to say that. I'm proud of our adventure. And we're still adventuring. With the ups, downs, and sideways we've kept it together. We have kids that are tough (21, 19, 15). Fighters. They keep getting better and that's fun to watch. It's not all perfect, but accepting that has been important.

I'm proud of Brandi. Four years ago she decided to do her own thing. She and her partner have built a mental health company. A few dozen therapists helping people get better. Focused mostly on helping military folks and their families. Building is hard. Gosh it's hard. But they are relentless. And care about each other. And the mission. And their humans. And I get to see them get better every day. That's what good teams do. Their focus on helping other people win is how they are going to win. I know it.

Today we're in New York City. With three dogs, a cat, and a tortoise. Yep. True story.

If you made it this far without whispering dude, stop, I owe you a coffee.
